# Description Style Guide — Origins of Value

A reference for writing and editing the `description` field in `data/museum-data.json`.
The goal is consistency: every reader should feel the entries were written by one curator
with one voice. Use this when authoring new descriptions or correcting existing ones.

> **Ground rule:** the JPEG image is the authority. Describe what is actually on the
> document. Never invent facts, translations, dates, names, or amounts that the image
> does not support. When something is unreadable, say so — do not guess on the page.

---

## 1. The three description types

The collection holds three kinds of records. Identify which one you are writing before
you start, because each has its own form.

> **The governing trade:** a description is interpretation, not a data dump. Cut the data a
> reader can't use (serial numbers above all) and spend those words on what a structured
> field can never capture — formal analysis of the visual motifs and interpretation of the
> document's economic moment — while keeping narrative backstory to a clause. *Lean on data,
> rich on interpretation, light on narrative history.*

| Type | When to use | Voice | Length |
|------|-------------|-------|--------|
| **A. Primary prose** | The main record for a document | Full sentences, encyclopedic | ~90–140 words* |
| **B. Catalogue fragment** | Reverse/conditions pages, coupon sheets, secondary pages of a multi-page item | Terse noun fragments | ~25–45 words |
| **C. Legacy essay** | Pre-existing long-form Goetzmann entries (0900, 0904, 0908, 0909, 0910) | Narrative history | Do not extend; see §6 |

\* Length is substance-driven, not a quota: spend words on formal and economic analysis, never on padding. Trim ruthlessly if the analysis is thin.

Type A is the default and the standard the collection aspires to. When in doubt, write Type A.

---

## 2. Type A — Primary prose (the standard)

### Structure
Three movements, in this order:

1. **Identification** — what the object *is*, plus the facts that carry meaning, in the
   first one or two sentences: instrument type, issuer, denomination/face value, interest
   rate, currency. Omit superfluous identifiers such as serial numbers (see §5, "What to
   include — and what to cut").
2. **Place and date** — where and when it was issued.
3. **Significance** — interpret what the document reveals about its **economic and financial
   moment**, for a non-specialist. Distinguish two kinds of context, because they are
   governed differently:
   - **Economic / financial interpretation is high-value — be rigorous, and feel free to go
     deeper.** Contextualize the instrument in its economy: *how* it reflects its moment
     (e.g. a 1920 Austrian treasury note read against post-war inflation and reconstruction
     finance), what its terms imply, how it sat in its market. **Why the instrument was
     issued** belongs here too — the fiscal pressure or monetary policy behind it. A sentence
     or two on, say, why Bulgaria floated a national-defense loan while aligned with the Axis,
     or on Tsar Boris III's war finance, sharpens the entry (goetzmann0003). This analysis is
     welcome and can carry real depth.
   - **Narrative history is bounded — keep it to a clause.** The issuer's biography, a
     chronicle of events, who-did-what-when: name only what's needed and move on. Don't turn
     the entry into a history essay (that is the legacy style we avoid; see §4). *(The
     **reasons** an instrument exists — war finance, fiscal crisis, monetary policy — are
     economic interpretation, not narrative history, and are welcome per the bullet above.
     What is bounded is the chronicle and the biography.)*
   - **Situate it in financial history.** When the instrument prefigures or exemplifies a
     broader development, say so — that is high-value context. *Example (goetzmann0340,
     Confederate cotton bond): backing a bond on a commodity rather than on the government's
     credit makes it an early cousin of the futures contract (formally traded on the Chicago
     Board of Trade from 1865) — a remarkable innovation, even though the loan failed.*
   - **Flag genuine rarity.** If the document is exceptional within the collection or its
     class, note what makes it so. *Example (goetzmann0677, Russian Liberty Loan): the only
     instrument here whose debt was repudiated and whose holders were never repaid.*

   In short: rich on economic interpretation, light on narrative backstory.

**Earn every interpretive claim.** Never assert significance you don't explain. If you write
that an instrument carried "a new ideological charge," say *why* in the same breath.
*Example (goetzmann0186, Continental lottery ticket): the charge was new because the ticket
was aimed at a broad public of merchants and tradespeople rather than nobility alone, tying
bond-buying to civic participation and patriotic duty.* A claim without its reason is a
fragment to finish, not to keep.

**Specify, don't gesture.** Naming a concept is the start of a sentence, not the end of one.
When you invoke a convention, practice, or category, say concretely what it consists of —
don't leave an empty phrase. *Example (goetzmann0019, Berbice asset inventory): "reflecting
the bookkeeping conventions of Dutch colonial enterprise" gestures; specifying earns it —
those conventions entered 895 enslaved people, categorised by size as "large and small" like
livestock, in the very same numbered ledger of assets as cannon, Fort Nassau, and a church,
each at an estimated guilder value, treating human beings as fungible capital assets.* If a
phrase could trail off into "…in some way," unpack it instead.

**Anchor interpretation to the object.** Identify before (or as) you interpret. A description
that is pure thesis — with no statement of what the document *is*, who made it, or when — fails;
the interpretation has nothing to attach to. *Example (goetzmann0181): a draft that opened on the
lottery's "popularity" and "culture building" but never said the sheet was a manuscript subscriber
list kept by the Winchester agent William Holliday left the reader a thesis and no document.* Lead
with the object, then let it carry the argument.

### Worked example (goetzmann0385, 93 words)
> *This printed bearer bond of 189 rubles (equivalent to £20 sterling) at 4½% annual
> interest was issued by the Kahetian Railway Company (Кахетинская железная дорога).
> No. A14533. St. Petersburg, 1912.* **← identification + place/date**
> *The Kahetian (Kakhetian) Railway served the Kakheti wine-growing region of eastern
> Georgia in the Caucasus, linking Tiflis (Tbilisi) with the wine-producing areas. The
> dual denomination in rubles and British pounds indicates the bond was marketed to both
> domestic and international investors. This document reflects the active development of
> Caucasian railway infrastructure by private companies in the Russian Empire during the
> early twentieth century.* **← significance**

*Note: this is an existing record quoted verbatim. Under the current guidance (§5) a new
description would drop the serial "No. A14533" as superfluous and reinvest that space in
interpretation.*

Other strong models: goetzmann0397 (Mexican Telephone Co.), goetzmann0398 (Minas Geraes
Goldfields), goetzmann0580 (quadrilingual Bulgarian gold loan), and especially
**goetzmann0011** (Middelburg maritime-insurance broadsheet) — a gold-standard entry that
reads *physical format into function* (a two-column broadsheet with dense text, meant for
public posting) and sets it in the 1720 speculative climate. Study it.

### Opening sentence
Lead with **"This [instrument] …"** for the primary prose form. The instrument phrase
should be specific: *"This printed bearer bond …"*, *"This engraved stock certificate …"*,
*"This printed share warrant to bearer …"*. Avoid generic openers like *"This document is a…"*.

## 3. Type B — Catalogue fragment (pages, reverses, coupons)

Used for the back of a document, conditions pages, coupon sheets, and the non-primary
pages of a multi-page item. These are deliberately terse: identify the parent document,
the salient data, and the page position. No historical essay.

> **The primary record — including page 1 of a multi-page item — is Type A, not Type B.**
> Don't leave the lead record as a bare fragment just because the object spans several pages;
> it earns the full identify → interpret treatment. (goetzmann0004's main `description` is the
> model; only its *page* entries are Type B.)

> **Policy (adopted) — describe every page from its own image.** Each entry in `pages[]` maps
> to one JPEG; open *that* image and describe what is actually on *that* page. Never carry
> imported catalogue text, and never repeat the parent's description across pages. A page
> entry may stay terse, but it must be a genuine, image-based observation — the specific
> articles, table, signatures, illustration, seal, or marginalia visible on that page. This
> supersedes any minimal "just strip the boilerplate" fix for the existing page arrays.

> **Some "multi-page items" are sets of distinct works, not pages of one document.** A
> 51-card satirical deck (goetzmann0028, the "John Law and South Sea Bubble" cards) is the
> clearest case: each card is its own engraving with its own scene, suit/rank inset, and
> satirical verse, lampooning a different bubble-era scheme. Each member earns a real
> image-based description *and* an interpretation (which scheme it mocks, what the verse
> says) — not a bare suit/rank label. The primary record characterises the **set and its
> genre**; each page describes its **individual work**.

> **Imported page metadata can be wrong, not merely sparse — verify from the image.** The
> leaked labels mislabel goetzmann0028 as the "Jack of Diamonds" when the card plainly shows
> the **King of Diamonds** (crowned figure, red diamond pip). Trust the JPEG, not the
> inherited label, for suit, rank, page order, and everything else.

### Worked examples
> *Reverse/conditions page of Province of Nova Scotia Government Redeemable Stock
> (No. C1515, £1,000 at 3½%), showing terms and conditions of the stock and additional
> coupon rows in red/orange. Page 3 of 4.* — goetzmann0986

> *Reverse of Banque Industrielle de Chine (中法實業銀行) 500-franc ordinary share
> (No. 258044), showing "Extraits des Statuts" (extracts of the bylaws) in French, and
> Chinese coupon dividend stubs (中法實業銀行息票) at bottom. Page 2 of 2.* — goetzmann0989

### Conventions
- Open with the role: **"Reverse of…"**, **"Reverse/conditions page of…"**, **"Coupon
  sheet for…"**, **"Verso (back) of…"**.
- Repeat just enough to tie the page to its parent (denomination, rate, and — only if
  needed to disambiguate otherwise-identical pages — a serial). The "cut superfluous data"
  rule still applies; don't list a serial out of habit.
- **Describe each page's own content — never identical boilerplate.** Each page record
  corresponds to **one image**; describe what is actually visible on *this* page (which
  articles, which table, which signatures, which illustration). If every page carries the
  same generic line, the text isn't a description at all — it's usually **imported
  catalogue metadata** that was never replaced with an image-based reading. Replace it.
  (goetzmann0004–0010 repeat one vague line; goetzmann0012–0018 repeat leaked MARC
  boilerplate — both are the anti-pattern.)
- End with **"Page X of Y."** when part of a multi-page item.
- Sentence fragments are acceptable here; full grammatical sentences are not required.

## 4. Type C — Legacy Goetzmann essays

Records 0900, 0904, 0908, 0909, 0910 carry long narrative histories in a distinct older
voice (all-caps city + date opener: *"MADRID. 1 June 1752. Accion de 500 escudos…"*).

- **Do not rewrite them into Type A**, and do not author new descriptions in this style.
- If you must correct a fact inside one, keep the surrounding voice intact and change only
  what is wrong.
- New records always use Type A (or Type B for pages).

## 5. House conventions (apply to all types)

### What to include — and what to cut
Every sentence should do work a structured metadata field cannot. Cut data that lives in
the metadata or that carries no meaning for the reader.

- **Cut superfluous identifiers — serial numbers, series, and class designations.** None of
  them tell the reader what the document *means*, and all are already captured in structured
  fields. Leave them out of the prose, along with any routine catalogue code that carries no
  interpretive weight. (goetzmann0003 carried both a series name and a serial number that
  should be cut.)
- **Keep the terms that carry meaning.** An interest rate is **not** superfluous — it
  signals the instrument's risk, its era, and the market it was sold into, so keep it.
  Likewise denomination, issuer, currency, and date.
- **Prefer data a financial researcher can use.** In place of catalogue identifiers, surface
  the terms that describe the instrument *as a financial product*: the **coupon/interest
  rate**, the **term to maturity** (how long the bond runs, when it is redeemable or
  callable), and any redemption/conversion conditions. Where it is relevant *and verifiable*,
  set the rate in context — e.g. against the era's prevailing central-bank or benchmark rate
  — to show whether its terms were generous or conservative for the time. Never invent a
  comparison figure (see §6).
- **Summarise granular detail — *unless the detail is the document's substance*.** Exhaustive
  enumerations that are *incidental* — routine line-item lists, every unit price, catalogue
  sub-figures — belong in the `transcription` field, not the description; repeating them is
  clutter, and the transcription preserves them in full (e.g. the tobacco line-items on the
  goetzmann0464 invoice are summarised to "two lots of plug tobacco"). **But keep granular
  detail when it defines what the document *is*:** a lottery proposal's prize table, a bond's
  core terms — there the figures *are* the substance, not clutter, so set them out in full
  (goetzmann0214 keeps its entire prize ladder and the blanks ratio). The test: is this detail
  the heart of what the document is, or incidental to it? Keep the former, summarise the
  latter. Either way, the figures that always stay are denomination, rate, headline scale or
  total, and a telling ratio.
- **Reinvest, don't just delete.** The space saved by cutting a serial should go toward
  interpretation and context (the subsections below), not be left empty. *Lean on data,
  rich on meaning.*

### Voice and tense
- Third person, present tense, neutral and encyclopedic — a museum wall label, not a sales
  listing. **No first person and no "we"**, and no direct address to the reader.
- **For interpretation, make the object the subject** — *"the frame reveals…"*, *"the motifs
  speak to…"* — or use an impersonal *"one sees…"*. This keeps the scholarly remove while
  still letting the entry interpret; it is the preferred alternative to *"we see…"*.
  *Example: not "We see how Art Nouveau permeated Viennese visual culture," but "The bond's
  frame reveals the reach of Art Nouveau, which by then had permeated Viennese visual culture."*
- State facts plainly. Reserve hedging words (*"suggests"*, *"likely"*, *"approximately"*,
  *"ca."*) for genuine inference, and use them when the inference is real.

### Foreign text and scripts
- Give the original script **once**, followed by an English gloss or translation in
  parentheses: *"Облигация (bearer bond)"*, *"Кахетинская железная дорога"*,
  *"中法實業銀行 (Sino-French Industrial Bank)"*.
- **Gloss selectively — don't reflexively parenthesize every translated noun.** Keep the
  original term only when it carries weight: a proper name, an institution, a technical or
  legal term, or a *telling* original phrase worth quoting (see "The document's own language").
  For ordinary nouns the English already covers, drop the gloss. *Example (goetzmann0019): the
  review cut "(effecten)" and the Dutch for "large and small" as clutter — "assets" and "large
  and small" carry the meaning on their own.*
- Provide a transliteration when it aids the reader: *"Tiflis (Tbilisi)"*.
- Preserve diacritics exactly: *Compañía*, *Díaz*, *Penaflorida*, *Borği*. Do not strip
  accents.
- Note period spellings where they differ from modern usage: *"Minas Gerais (often spelled
  'Minas Geraes' in period documents)"*.

### Dates
- Format full dates as **"Month D, YYYY"** (*February 28, 1905*) or **"Place, YYYY"**
  (*St. Petersburg, 1912*).
- Dual-calendar dates use a slash, older/newer: *"10/23 May 1905"* (Julian/Gregorian).
- When a year in the document's title is a **loan name** rather than the issue date, say
  so: *"Dated Sofia, 10/23 May 1905 (the 1904 in the loan title is the loan name)."*
- For inferred dates, use *"ca."* or *"approximately"* and name the basis in `notes`.
- **When the date is inferred from context rather than read off the document, make the
  inference visible and hedge it** — don't state or imply a firm issue date you cannot see.
  Show the reasoning, then soften the claim. *Example (goetzmann0011): not "during the
  speculative climate of 1720" (which reads as a known date), but "Middelburg's prominence as
  a centre for maritime insurance during the speculative climate of 1720 dates the document to
  around this era; hence it is likely associated with the 1720 bubble."* The date now reads as
  an inference, not a reading. (Complement to §6: when the document **does** state its date,
  use that and don't let the collection override it.)

### Money, denominations, rates
- Use the document's own currency and symbols: *£*, *$*, francs, rubles, pesos, escudos,
  leva, kyat. Keep fractions as *½ ¼ ⅓ ¾* and rates as *4½%*, *3½%*.
- British amounts: *£17.10s.*, *£3.0.0*, *15 pounds 17 shillings*.
- Record **dual denominations** when present: *"189 rubles (equivalent to £20 sterling)"*.
- The denomination on the face is the source of truth — verify it against the image
  (see the goetzmann0002 Burmese bond, where the catalogued "50 / 5%" was actually
  **Ten Kyat / ၁၀**).

### Identifiers
- **Keep serial numbers, series, and class designations out of the prose** — they are
  catalogue data, already held in structured fields, and add nothing to a reader's
  understanding (see "What to include — and what to cut" above).
- Keep only the terms that describe the instrument's *meaning*: denomination, interest rate,
  currency, term to maturity.
- Cross-reference a sibling document by its catalogue id, not its serial: *"Same issue as
  goetzmann0715."*

### Visual motifs — describe, then interpret
Strong visual description is necessary but not sufficient. After describing distinctive
decoration, **interpret it** with genuine formal analysis: what do the motifs reveal about
the aesthetic and material culture of their time and place? This is one of the
highest-value things a description can do — go deep here.

- **Move from description to formal analysis to interpretation.** Don't stop at naming a
  motif; analyse its style and connect it to the broader visual movement it belongs to, then
  say what that reveals. *Example (goetzmann0001, Austrian treasury note): the green and
  pink floral scrollwork is not mere ornament — its sinuous, organic line ties the note to
  the Art Nouveau movement then sweeping European design, showing how contemporary
  developments in visual culture shaped even official financial communications.*
- **Name the movement or influence** when you can identify it (Art Nouveau, Baroque,
  neoclassical, Art Deco, French arabesque, Roman/Greek revival) and what its presence
  implies. *Example (goetzmann0002, the Burmese bond): the French arabesque scrollwork and
  Roman laurel-wreath motifs framing an all-Burmese-script independence bond reveal how
  deeply Western visual culture had taken root in Burma after colonization.*
- Treat the document as an artifact of **material culture and "financial communications"** —
  a designed object that signals authority and aspiration. Connect form to meaning: imperial
  eagles, allegorical figures, national emblems, and the choice of script or ornament all
  carry political identity.
- **Read physical format into function, not just ornament.** The object's layout is evidence
  too. *Example (goetzmann0011, Middelburg maritime-insurance conditions): the two-column
  broadsheet format and dense text mark it as meant for public posting or distribution to
  prospective investors — its physical form reveals its public purpose.*
- **Push the analysis to a thesis.** Let the visual reading carry an argument about the
  moment. *Example (goetzmann0454, Eighth Austrian War Loan, 1918): the Art Nouveau styling
  and imperial typography are the visual language of a dying empire — Habsburg grandeur
  performed on a war loan issued months before the monarchy dissolved.*
- **Fact-check every art-historical attribution before asserting it.** Naming an influence is
  a factual claim, not a flourish. *Example (goetzmann0003, Bulgarian defense loan): one might
  read a Turkish / Islamic-manuscript-illumination influence in the geometric motifs — a vivid
  illustration of the blurred exchange of visual culture between East and West — but assert it
  only after verifying the attribution actually holds.* Plausible is not the same as true.
- Note cancellation and physical markings too: *"Stamped CANCELLED"*, *"ink-cancelled with
  a large 'X'"*, vellum, paper seals.
- Interpret, but never fabricate — every reading must be grounded in what is actually on
  the document.

### The document's own language
When the wording on the document is itself revealing, quote a short, telling phrase and
analyse its register — the rhetoric is evidence.

- *Example (goetzmann0677, Russian Liberty Loan): the appeal's desperate, almost poetic
  register — "In the name of citizens of the great, free Russia, by those to whom the future
  of our Motherland is dear, we make this ardent appeal" — conveys the Provisional
  Government's precarious bid for legitimacy more vividly than any paraphrase.*
- Keep quotations short; translate foreign-language quotations and give the original where it
  matters.

### People and entities
- Name signatories and grantees when legible, with role: *"Signed by the Bulgarian Finance
  Minister T. Baiçounov (Байчунов) and the Director of the Public Debt."*
- Give modern equivalents and one-line context for institutions, rulers, and eras:
  *"the Porfiriato era (the long presidency of Porfirio Díaz, 1876–1911)"*.

### Punctuation and typography
- En dash for ranges and year spans: *1876–1911*, *Page 2 of 4*. Em dash (—) for asides.
- Use straight ASCII where the data pipeline expects it, but non-ASCII letters and original
  scripts are fully supported and **must be preserved** (the stored values are proper
  Unicode even when a console renders them as "�").

## 6. Accuracy and sourcing (do not skip)

- **The image is ground truth.** Where a description and the JPEG disagree, the JPEG wins;
  fix the description.
- **Do not assert what you cannot read.** Use *"[surname illegible]"*, *"(text illegible
  in scan)"*, *"number not legible in image"* rather than inventing a plausible value.
- **Do not fabricate** translations, clauses, attributions, or context that the document
  does not contain. (E.g. a removed invented "Aniéni region" clause on goetzmann0370; the
  removed bogus "(1/496 of the undertaking)" on goetzmann0912.)
- **Watch recurring traps:** Confederate bonds are executed by *the Register of the
  Treasury*, not the Secretary; Continental loan bills are signed *Fra. Hopkinson*, not
  "H. Atkinson". When a class of documents shares a misread, fix the whole class.
- **Beware contextual contamination — don't inherit a neighbour's or a collection's date or
  theme.** Verify each document's own date and subject from the document itself. An item bound
  into a later compilation may predate it: goetzmann0020 is a **1705** anti-Barbary-piracy
  *Project*, but its description pattern-matched to the surrounding **1720** Groote Tafereel
  and wrongly tied it to "the speculative project culture of the 1720 period." Adjacent
  records share a binding, not necessarily a date or a subject. A multi-page item where only
  page 1 is legible is especially prone to this — describe the date you can verify, don't
  borrow one from the shelf.
- **Don't bend the object's function to fit the collection's theme; interpretation must not
  outrun the facts.** Describe what the instrument actually *is* before fitting it to a
  headline narrative. A *Wisselbank* (exchange bank, goetzmann0012) is a **stabilising**
  deposit-and-giro institution modelled on the Amsterdam Wisselbank — not a speculative
  joint-stock company — so framing it as the state "promoting the speculative frenzy"
  conflates two different things. And verify institutional facts: the Dutch Republic's banks
  were chartered by **municipal or provincial** governments (Utrecht's by its Burgemeesteren
  en Vroedschap), **not by a "sovereign crown"** — the Republic had none. A rich interpretation
  built on a misread function is still wrong. The imposed theme need not be the collection's —
  it can be the writer's own analytical lens: goetzmann0020 (the anti-Barbary-piracy *Project*)
  concerns Dutch Mediterranean navigation and commerce and *Turkish/Barbary* pirates, so reading
  it through a "colonial networks of trade" frame, or calling the corsairs "Dutch pirates,"
  bends the document toward a thesis it doesn't state. Let the interpretation follow the
  document, not the other way round.
- **Beware anachronism — don't project later concepts onto earlier documents.** Terms like
  "industrial society," "capitalism," or "the nation-state" carry their own histories;
  applied to a document that predates them, they mislead. goetzmann0028 (the 1720 "Bubble"
  cards) belongs to a **pre-industrial** commercial economy of joint-stock companies and
  public credit — not "industrial society" (the Industrial Revolution is decades away). Name
  the institutions and vocabulary the document's own era would recognise. This includes
  **modern academic jargon**: render "third places" as the actual taverns, coffee-houses, and
  parlours; "social capital" as the actual networks — use the period's concrete terms, not
  later theory-speak.
- Put the basis for any inferred date or fact in the `notes` field, not buried in the
  description.

### Data hygiene
- **Strip leaked escape artifacts.** Watch for `_x000B_` (a vertical-tab escape from Excel)
  and any `_xNNNN_` sequence — these are not content and must be removed (replace with a
  space or sentence break). They are widespread in the `pages[]` descriptions.
- **Strip leaked source-catalogue metadata.** Imported library records dump collection-level
  boilerplate into the text — binding/holdings notes (*"57 printed and 6 ms. items bound
  together under title page"*), field labels (*"Author: Utrecht (Netherlands)"*), and repeated
  collection titles. None of it describes the item in hand; remove it. A genuine
  collection-level fact (e.g. that the bound volume holds 57 printed + 6 manuscript items)
  belongs, if anywhere, in the **primary** record's context or `notes` — never copied onto
  every page. (goetzmann0012–0018 are the anti-pattern.)
- **Fix transcription typos**, e.g. "Perspectus" → "Prospectus". Spell-check proper nouns and
  instrument names against their correct forms.
- Never let an em-dash, line break, or markup escape stand in as text — clean the raw value.

### Transcriptions & translations
- **A transcription's English translation must be coherent English.** Machine/OCR
  translations that leave untranslated foreign fragments and nonsense tokens (goetzmann0004:
  *"cheeit-vogtoot", "fluyers", "Adic", "thuiyers"*) are not acceptable — flag them and
  regenerate, or translate manually.
- **Translate from the image, not from the OCR.** The underlying OCR of early-modern type is
  often itself corrupt, so a translation built on it inherits the garbage. Work from the page
  image; where the source is genuinely illegible, mark *[illegible]* rather than emit nonsense.

## 7. Quick checklist before saving a description

- [ ] Correct type (A primary / B fragment / C leave-as-is)?
- [ ] Type A opens with *"This [specific instrument] …"* and follows identify → place/date → significance?
- [ ] Instrument, issuer, denomination, and rate present and matching the image?
- [ ] **Superfluous data cut** — no serial, series, or class in the prose; researcher-relevant terms (rate, term to maturity, redemption conditions) surfaced instead?
- [ ] **Rate set in context** where relevant and verifiable (vs benchmark/central-bank rate)?
- [ ] **Visual motifs given formal analysis** — style named, tied to an art movement, interpreted (not just described) — and any attribution fact-checked?
- [ ] **Economic interpretation present and rigorous**, while narrative backstory kept to a clause?
- [ ] **Every interpretive claim explained** — no significance asserted without its "why"?
- [ ] **No vague gestures** — every convention/concept invoked is specified concretely, not left as an empty phrase?
- [ ] **Granular detail handled right** — incidental enumerations summarised (to transcription), but substance-defining detail (prize tables, core terms) kept in full?
- [ ] Distinctive document wording quoted where it is revealing?
- [ ] **Title** (if edited): issuer/instrument + place + date; no denomination, serial, series, or "(Page X of Y)" — see §8?
- [ ] Foreign text glossed **selectively** (not every noun parenthesized); diacritics and scripts preserved?
- [ ] Dates formatted correctly; loan-name-vs-issue-date disambiguated?
- [ ] **Inferred dates hedged and shown as inference** (not stated as a firm reading) when the document doesn't give a date?
- [ ] Nothing asserted that the image doesn't show; illegible items flagged, not guessed?
- [ ] **Date/theme verified from the document itself**, not inherited from neighbouring records or the collection?
- [ ] **No anachronism** — no later concept (industrial society, capitalism, nation-state) projected onto a document that predates it?
- [ ] Type B fragment ends with *"Page X of Y."* if multi-page, and describes *this* page's content (not repeated boilerplate)?
- [ ] **No leaked artifacts** (`_x000B_` / `_xNNNN_`) or transcription typos (e.g. "Perspectus") in the value?
- [ ] Length substance-driven (A ≈ 90–140 words, B ≈ 25–45) — earned by analysis, not padding?

## 8. Titles (the related `title` field)

Though this guide is about descriptions, the same "cut superfluous data" instinct governs
titles. Keep them short and human.

**Formula:** *[Issuer / instrument name] + [place, if helpful] + [date].*

- **Be specific — a title must identify the object, not just gesture at it.** Name what the
  thing *is* (its type and subject), not a bare rhetorical phrase. A document's own oblique
  title may need replacing: *"How the Insurance Company of Rotterdam Can Be Promoted…"*
  (goetzmann0004) reads as vague because nothing signals what the object is — the curator's
  fix names it directly: **"Insurance Company of Rotterdam Charter Documents."**
- **Include** the recognizable issuer/instrument and, where they aid identification, the
  place and the date (a year, or a full date for war loans). A defining recipient may stand
  in for other detail: *"State of Massachusetts Bay War Bond issued to Seth Davenport 1777."*
- **Cut** denomination, interest rate, serial number, series, class, and original-script
  names — they belong in structured fields, not the title. *"Russian Provisional Government
  Liberty Loan, Petrograd, 1917"* — not *"…5% Bond, 100 Rubles, IV Series, No. 0643022…"*.
- **No "(Page X of Y)" in a title.** A title names the document, not its pagination; check
  it isn't carrying a stray page marker (goetzmann0226 did).

> **This updates the earlier title convention that stripped *all* dates.** Place and date are
> now welcome in titles; denominations, serials, series, and class qualifiers still are not.

Worked title edits applied with this guidance: goetzmann0186, 0226, 0383, 0454, 0677.

## 9. Before & After — worked transformations

Complete examples of the house style applied to real records. Watch how each revision spends
its words: it cuts catalogue data and reinvests the room in interpreting the object's visual
language.

### Example 1 — goetzmann0002 (Burmese government bond)

**Before:**
> A Burmese government bond written entirely in Burmese script, featuring green and purple
> decorative borders with traditional floral and geometric motifs. The document displays the
> serial number 008358 in red numerals on both sides and a central circular vignette with the
> denomination printed in Burmese characters. The footer contains a date in the Burmese
> calendar system, suggesting issuance during the 1940s when Burma operated as a nominally
> independent state under Japanese occupation. The design incorporates traditional Burmese
> decorative elements including intricate petal and leaf motifs framing the central text, in a
> style consistent with official financial instruments of the era. This is a rare surviving
> example of financial documentation from wartime Burma, notable for its exclusive use of the
> indigenous script at a politically turbulent moment in Burmese history.

**After:**
> A Burmese government bond written in native Burmese script. The note is adorned with green
> and purple decorative borders replete with floral and arabesque laurel motifs. The visual
> language of the note communicates a story of self-determination and syncretic identity. The
> design incorporates traditional Burmese decorative elements — intricate petal and leaf
> motifs framing the central text — in a style consistent with European formal financial
> instruments of the era. The juxtaposition of Burmese script alongside European visual motifs
> of arabesque swirls and laurel wreaths speaks to the assertion of a uniquely post-colonial
> Burmese identity. The footer carries a date in the Burmese calendar, suggesting issuance in
> the 1940s, when Burma operated as a nominally independent state under Japanese occupation,
> following decades of British colonial rule. This is a rare surviving example of financial
> documentation from wartime Burma, notable for its exclusive use of the indigenous script at a
> politically turbulent moment in Burmese history.

**What changed, and why:**
- **Cut the serial number** ("008358 … on both sides") — superfluous catalogue data (§5,
  "What to include — and what to cut").
- **Ornament elevated from described to *interpreted*.** The same green/purple borders now
  carry a thesis — East–West syncretism and post-colonial self-assertion — instead of merely
  being catalogued (§5, "Visual motifs — push to a thesis").
- **The visual vocabulary is named precisely** — European arabesque swirls and laurel wreaths
  set against indigenous Burmese script (§5). *Fact-check such attributions before asserting
  them (§5); they read as confident claims.*
- **One bounded line of context added** — "following decades of British colonial rule" — for
  the non-specialist, without sliding into a history essay (§2).

> **When this revision is actually written into goetzmann0002, merge the factual corrections
> from the image review** that the stylistic draft above still omits: denomination **Ten Kyat
> (၁၀)** (not "50"), the national slogan **"One Blood, One Voice, One Command"
> (တသွေး·တသံ·တမိန့်)**, and the Burmese-era date **BE 1305 = 1943/44**. Style and accuracy
> both, or neither. *(The After text above is reproduced from the curator's revision, lightly
> tidied for punctuation.)*

### Example 2 — goetzmann0001 (Austrian 5,000-Kronen treasury note)

**Before:**
> A bearer treasury note issued by the Austrian state for 5,000 Kronen, dated September 1,
> 1920 in Vienna, bearing serial number 008255 in Series 1920b. The note is designated as
> tax-free (steuerfrei) and interest-bearing, with provisions for redemption by the state debt
> administration. The document features elaborate green and pink decorative borders with floral
> motifs, a double-headed Austrian imperial eagle at the top center, and ornamental medallions
> at each corner. Issued in the immediate aftermath of World War I and the dissolution of the
> Habsburg Empire, this bond represents the new Austrian republic's efforts to raise capital
> during a period of severe economic instability and hyperinflation.

**After:**
> A Viennese bearer treasury note issued by the Austrian state in 1920 for 5,000 Kronen, this
> note is designated as tax-free (steuerfrei) and interest-bearing, with provisions for
> redemption by the state debt administration. The document features elaborate green and pink
> decorative borders with floral motifs, a double-headed Austrian imperial eagle at the top
> center, and ornamental medallions at each corner. The variegated, opulent, vibrant swirls of
> the bond's frame speak to the influence of the Art Nouveau movement: the note's design reveals
> how the visual language of Art Nouveau, having found a stronghold in Europe, had permeated the
> visual culture of the Viennese state. Issued in the immediate aftermath of World War I and the
> dissolution of the Habsburg Empire, this bond represents the new Austrian republic's efforts
> to raise capital during a period of severe economic instability and hyperinflation. This is
> further in conversation with the values of opulence, wealth, and extravagance espoused by the
> aesthetic values of Art Nouveau rippling throughout Europe at the time.

**What changed, and why:**
- **Cut the serial number and series** ("008255 … Series 1920b") — superfluous catalogue data
  (§5).
- **Place folded into the opener** ("A *Viennese* bearer treasury note … in 1920") — tighter
  identification (§2 / §8).
- **The decorative frame elevated to an Art Nouveau thesis** — the swirls now read as the
  movement's spread into Viennese visual culture, not just "floral motifs" (§5, "name the
  movement" + "push to a thesis").

**Two things to watch in this draft** (teaching points):
- **Voice.** The curator's draft read *"We see how the visual language of Art Nouveau…"*. Per
  the resolved §5 rule, first person / "we" is revised to the **object-as-subject** form
  (*"the note's design reveals how…"*) — shown applied in the After text above. Interpretive,
  but without the first person.
- **Fact-check the attribution.** The curator's draft called it the *"prescient* influence of
  Art Nouveau" — debatable, since by 1920 the movement was already giving way to Art Deco, so
  the styling reads as backward-looking, not prescient. The After text drops "prescient"
  accordingly (§5, "fact-check every art-historical attribution"). Art Nouveau is also invoked
  three times in the draft; one developed pass would read tighter ("earned by analysis, not
  padding").
