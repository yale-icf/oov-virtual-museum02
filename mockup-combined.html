About the Collection

The Origins of Value collection comprises 545 historical financial documents — bonds, stock certificates, banknotes, prospectuses, and more — spanning the 17th through 20th centuries and drawn from 65 countries. Assembled at the International Center for Finance at the Yale School of Management, it traces the evolution of financial instruments from the earliest share certificates of Dutch colonial companies to twentieth-century securities issued across Europe, Asia, and the Americas.

Representing 77 languages and 131 currencies, each document tells a story of commerce, innovation, and risk. Together, they form a remarkable record of how societies have organized capital, financed exploration, and weathered financial crises from the South Sea Bubble to the modern era.

Tontines

A tontine was a collective annuity: investors pooled capital and drew income for life, with each death increasing the share of survivors. Invented in 17th-century France, they were among history’s most ingenious — and morbid — financial instruments.

Featured Item

VOC Obligation Receipt

Dutch East India Company — Amsterdam, 1622
The world’s first multinational corporation, financed by thousands of individual investors who received printed receipts like this one.

Issued just twenty years after the founding of the Vereenigde Oost-Indische Compagnie, this obligation receipt represents an early form of corporate debt — a promise to repay a subscriber who had funded the Company’s voyages to the East Indies. The VOC was the first company to offer shares to the general public, laying the groundwork for modern capital markets.
