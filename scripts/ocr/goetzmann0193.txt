Those who have hitherto written on the English system of finance (the funding system) have been uniformly impressed with the idea of its downfall happening *some time or other.* They took, however, no data for that opinion, but expressed it predicatively, or merely as opinion, from a conviction that the perpetual duration of such a system was a natural impossibility. It is in this manner that Doctor Price has spoken of it; and Smith in his Wealth of Nations has spoken in the same manner; that is, merely as opinion without data. "The progress, says Smith, of the enormous debts, which at present oppresses and will in the long run *may probably ruin,* all the great nations of Europe, (though it might make some suppositions carried with it no conviction.

It is not my intention to predict any thing but I will throw from data already known, from hypotheses and facts, which the English funding system has already exhibited to, that it will not continue to the end of Mr. Pitt's life, supposing him to live the the age of a man. How much sooner it may fall, I leave to others to predict.

Let financiers diversify systems of credit as they will, it is nevertheless true, that every token of credit is a system of paper money. Two experiments have already been had upon paper money's the only one in both these cafes the America the other in France. In both cases the whole capital was emitted, and that whole capital, which in America was called continental money, and in France assignats, appeared in circulation; the consequence of which was, that the quantity became

The fame fate would have happened to gold and silver; could gold and silver could only have been iffued in the fame abundant manner as paper had been, and confined within the country as paper always is; by having no depreciation out of it; or, to speak on a larger scale, the fame thing would happen in the world, could the world be glutted with gold and silver, as America and France have been with paper.

The English system differs from that of America and France in this one particular, that its capital is kept out of light; that is, it does not appear in circulation. Were the whole capital of the national debt, which at the time I write this is about four hundred million pounds sterling, to be emitted in assignats or bills, and that whole quantity put into circulation, as was done in America and France, those English assignats, or bills, would sink in value as those of America and France have done with said bills in a greater degree, because the quantity of their would be more disproportioned to the quantity of population in England, than was the cafe in America or the other two countries. A nominal pound sterling in such bills would not be worth one penny.

But though the English system, by thus keeping the capital out of light, is preferred from [an] early destruction, as in the cafe of America and France, it nevertheless approaches the fame fate, and will arrive at it with the fame certainty, though by a flower progress.
