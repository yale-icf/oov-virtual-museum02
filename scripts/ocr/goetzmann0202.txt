Mr. Pitt continually talks of credit and of the national resources. These are two of the felonious appearances by which the approaches to bankruptcy are concealed. That which he calls credit may exist, as I have just shewn, in a state of insolvency, and is always what I have before described it to be, *sufficient offset.*

As to national reforces, Mr. Pitt, like all the English financiers that preceded him since the funding system began, his uniformly mistaken the nature of a resorce; that is, they have mistaken it confidently with the delusion of the funding system; but time is explaining the delusion. That which he calls, and which they called, a reforce, is not a reforce, but is the *anticipation of a reforce.* They have anticipated *what would have been a* resorce in another generation, had not the ule of it been to anticipated. The funding system is a system of anticipations. Those who established it an hundred years ago, anticipated the resources of Rhode who were to live a hundred years after; for the people of the present day have to pay the interest of the debts contracted at that time, and of all debts contracted since. But it is the half feather that breaks the horse's back. Do we not see that nature, in all her operations, throws the visionary bafis upon which the funding system is built. She acts always by renewed successions, and never by accumulating additions perpetually prografting. Animals and vegetables, men and trees, have exited ever since the world began; but that existence has been carried on by successions of generations, and not by continuing the same men and the same trees in existence that exited inti; and to make room for the new, the removes the old. Every natural idiot can see this. It is the fledgeling idiot only that mistakes. He has conceived that are can do what nature cannot. He is teaching her a new system.—That there is no occasion for man to die.—That the scheme of creation can be carried on upon the plan of the funding system.—That it can proceed by continual additions of new beings, like new loans, and all live together in eternal youth. Go, count the graves, thou idiot, and learn the folly of thy arithmetic.
