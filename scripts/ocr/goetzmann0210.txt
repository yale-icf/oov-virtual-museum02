A few days ago I transmitted to you, by the ordinary mode of distribution, a short Treatise, entitled "Dissertation on the First Principles of Government." This little work I did intend to have dedicated to the people of Holland, who, about the time I began to write it, were determined to accomplish a Revolution in their Government,—rather than to the people of France, who had long before effected that glorious object. But there are, in the Constitution which is about to be ratified by the Convention, certain articles, and in the report which preceded it, certain points, to repugnant to reason, and incompatible with the true principles of liberty, as to render this Treatise, drawn up for another purpose, applicable to the present occasion, and under this impression I presumed to submit it to your consideration.

If there be faults in the Constitution, it were better to expose them now, than to abide the event of their mischievous tendency; for certain it is, that the plan of the Constitution which has been presented to you is not confilient with the grand object of the Revolution; nor congenial to the sentiments of the individuals who accomplished it.

To deprive half the people in a nation of their rights as citizens, is as easy matter in theory or on paper; but it is a moist dangerous experiment, and rarely practicable in the execution.

I shall now, proceed to the observations I have to offer on this important subject; and I pledge myself that they shall be neither numerous nor dilative.

In my apprehension, a constitution contains two distinct parts or objects, the *Principle* and the *Practice*; and it is not only an efficient, but an indispensable provision, that the practice should emanate from, and accord with the principle. Now I maintain, that the entire of this proposition is the case in the plan of the Constitution under discussion. The first article, for instance, of the POLITICAL STATE OF CITIZENS, (v. TITLE II. OF THE CONSTITUTION) [says]:

"Every man born and resident in France, who, being twenty-one years of age, has inscribed on the Register of his Canton, and who has lived afterwards one year on the territory of the Republic, and who pays any direct contribution whatsoever, real or personal, is a French citizen."

I might here ask, if those only who come under the above description are to be confounded as citizens, what designation do you mean to give the rest of the people? I allude to that portion of the people on whom the principal part of the labour falls, and on whom the weight of indirect taxation will in the first instance press. In the furniture of the local fabric, this class of people are infinitely superior to that of the whole body qualification is their wealth or territorial possessions. For what is trade without cultivation? And what is the produce of the land without manufacturers? But to turn to the subject.

In the first place, this article is incompatible with the three first articles of the Declaration of Rights, which precede the Constitutional part.

The first article of the Declaration of Rights [says:—

"The end of society is the public good; and the institution of government is to secure to every individual the enjoyment of his rights."
