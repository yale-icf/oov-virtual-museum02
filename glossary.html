Document Glossary

Definitions of the financial instrument types represented in the collection

Bond

A debt instrument in which the issuer — typically a government, municipality, or corporation — borrows capital from the holder and promises to repay the principal at a specified maturity date while making regular interest payments. Bonds have financed wars, colonial expansion, and infrastructure for centuries and represent one of the oldest forms of standardized financial obligation. The collection includes government bonds from dozens of countries spanning the 17th through 20th centuries.

Stock Certificate

A document certifying ownership of shares in a joint-stock company, entitling the holder to a proportional claim on the company’s assets and profits. The Dutch East India Company (VOC) issued the first recognized share certificates in 1606, pioneering the model of dispersed corporate ownership that underpins modern capital markets. Stock certificates in this collection span colonial trading companies, national railways, banks, and industrial enterprises.

Browse stock certificates in the collection →

Certificate

A broad category of document attesting to a financial right or entitlement. In the collection, certificates include instruments representing interests in government funds, subscription rights, deposit receipts, annuity claims, and other obligations not captured by more specific instrument types. The term reflects the varied and experimental nature of early financial contracting, in which issuers devised bespoke instruments to meet particular funding needs.

Browse certificates in the collection →

Banknote

A promissory note issued by a bank or government and payable to the bearer on demand, functioning as paper currency. Banknotes eliminated the need to carry coin and, once widely accepted, became the primary medium of everyday exchange. Early notes were issued by private banks alongside state mints; central banks gradually assumed this role through the 19th and 20th centuries. The collection includes notes from state-sponsored banks, colonial institutions, and national treasuries.

Browse banknotes in the collection →

Coupon

A detachable slip attached to a bearer bond or other security, presented at specified intervals to claim an interest or dividend payment. The name derives from the French couper (to cut): holders would physically cut coupons from their bonds and redeem them at a bank or paying agent. Many bonds in the collection retain their coupon sheets, offering a tangible record of the payment schedule and the life of the instrument.

Browse coupons in the collection →

Tontine

A collective investment scheme in which subscribers contribute to a shared fund and receive annual payments; as members die, their income share is redistributed among the survivors, with the last survivor eventually claiming the full fund. Named after Neapolitan banker Lorenzo de Tonti, tontines were widely used by European governments in the 17th and 18th centuries as a means of raising long-term capital, blurring the boundary between investment and life insurance.

Browse tontines in the collection →

Prospectus

A formal disclosure document issued by a company or government to describe a proposed investment opportunity, including its purpose, financial terms, risks, and expected returns. Prospectuses solicited subscriptions from the public and are the precursors of the legally mandated securities disclosures required under modern financial regulation. Those in the collection reveal the promotional language and speculative ambitions of their eras.
