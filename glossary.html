Document Glossary

Definitions of the financial instrument types represented in the collection

Banknote

A promissory note issued by a bank or government and payable to the bearer on demand, functioning as paper currency. Banknotes eliminated the need to carry coin and, once widely accepted, became the primary medium of everyday exchange. Early notes were issued by private banks alongside state mints; central banks gradually assumed this role through the 19th and 20th centuries. The collection includes notes from state-sponsored banks, colonial institutions, and national treasuries.

Browse banknotes in the collection →

Bond

A debt instrument in which the issuer — typically a government, municipality, or corporation — borrows capital from the holder and promises to repay the principal at a specified maturity date while making regular interest payments. Bonds have financed wars, colonial expansion, and infrastructure for centuries and represent one of the oldest forms of standardized financial obligation. The collection includes government bonds from dozens of countries spanning the 17th through 20th centuries.

Browse bonds in the collection →

Certificate

A broad category of document attesting to a financial right or entitlement. In the collection, certificates include instruments representing interests in government funds, subscription rights, deposit receipts, annuity claims, and other obligations not captured by more specific instrument types. The term reflects the varied and experimental nature of early financial contracting, in which issuers devised bespoke instruments to meet particular funding needs.

Browse certificates in the collection →

Check

A written order instructing a bank to pay a specified sum from the drawer’s account to a named payee or to the bearer. As a payment instrument, the check became widespread in the 19th century alongside the growth of commercial banking and the substitution of paper for coin in everyday commerce. Checks in the collection document individual financial transactions between investors, banks, and institutions.

Browse checks in the collection →

Correspondence

Letters and written communications between financial institutions, investors, or agents. Correspondence in the collection illuminates the personal and commercial relationships behind financial transactions, revealing negotiation, instruction, and dispute in their original form. Such documents offer a rare view of financial activity as it was experienced day to day, beyond the formal face of printed instruments.

Browse correspondence in the collection →

Coupon

A detachable slip attached to a bearer bond or other security, presented at specified intervals to claim an interest or dividend payment. The name derives from the French couper (to cut): holders would physically cut coupons from their bonds and redeem them at a bank or paying agent. Many bonds in the collection retain their coupon sheets, offering a tangible record of the payment schedule and the life of the instrument.

Browse coupons in the collection →

Debt

A broad category encompassing instruments that document an obligation owed by one party to another. Items catalogued under this type represent obligations that do not fit more specific instrument categories, capturing the diversity of credit relationships in historical financial practice — from informal personal loans to complex state obligations.

Browse debt instruments in the collection →

Draft

A written order directing one party (the drawee) to pay a specified sum to another party (the payee) on a given date or on demand. Also known as a bill of exchange, the draft was a cornerstone of international trade finance from the medieval period onward, allowing merchants to settle debts across borders without physically moving coin. Drafts in the collection were issued by trading companies, colonial agents, and private banks.

Browse drafts in the collection →

Illustration

Printed images, engravings, or satirical prints relating to financial events or institutions. The collection includes illustrated broadsides and playing cards produced during the speculative manias of 1720, which used visual satire to comment on the South Sea Company, the Mississippi Company, and other bubble ventures. These images document public reaction to financial innovation and crisis as it unfolded.

Browse illustrations in the collection →

Indenture

A formal legal agreement between two or more parties, historically produced in duplicate and cut along a jagged (indented) edge so that each party retained a matching half as proof of authenticity. In financial contexts, indentures governed the terms of bond issues and lending arrangements, specifying interest rates, repayment schedules, collateral, and the rights of bondholders in case of default.

Browse indentures in the collection →

Invoice

A commercial document issued by a seller to a buyer listing goods or services provided and the amount owed. Financial invoices in the collection record transactions between merchants, banks, and trading companies, providing evidence of the goods and credit flows that underpinned early capital markets. They offer a granular view of the commercial relationships within which formal financial instruments operated.

Browse invoices in the collection →

Pamphlet

A short, unbound publication circulated to inform or persuade a public audience on a financial topic. Pamphlets in the collection include prospectus-like promotional texts, commentary on market conditions, and polemical tracts responding to speculative episodes such as the South Sea Bubble of 1720. As one of the primary media of financial discourse in the 18th century, pamphlets reveal how ideas about investment, risk, and credit were formed and contested.

Browse pamphlets in the collection →

Promissory Note

An unconditional written promise by one party (the maker) to pay a fixed sum to another party (the payee) at a specified time or on demand. Simpler in form than a bill of exchange, the promissory note is a direct acknowledgment of debt and one of the earliest standardized credit instruments. Notes in the collection document private loans, commercial credit, and colonial finance.

Browse promissory notes in the collection →

Prospectus

A formal disclosure document issued by a company or government to describe a proposed investment opportunity, including its purpose, financial terms, risks, and expected returns. Prospectuses solicited subscriptions from the public and are the precursors of the legally mandated securities disclosures required under modern financial regulation. Those in the collection reveal the promotional language and speculative ambitions of their eras.

Browse prospectuses in the collection →

Receipt

A written acknowledgment that a specified sum of money, goods, or securities has been received. Financial receipts in the collection document payments made on bonds, dividends collected, and deposits held, offering granular evidence of how instruments were administered and settled in practice. They preserve the transactional texture of financial life that formal instruments alone do not capture.

Browse receipts in the collection →

Register

A formal record documenting the holders of securities, transfers of ownership, or other financial transactions. Register pages in the collection offer direct evidence of how issuers tracked ownership before the era of electronic settlement, often recording transfers by hand in ledger format. They illuminate the administrative infrastructure that made large-scale securities markets possible.

Browse registers in the collection →

Security

A broad term for any tradable financial instrument representing an ownership stake (equity), a creditor relationship (debt), or rights to buy or sell another instrument. Items catalogued as securities in the collection include instruments that do not fit neatly into narrower categories, reflecting the fluid and evolving terminology of historical financial markets.

Browse securities in the collection →

Stock Certificate

A document certifying ownership of shares in a joint-stock company, entitling the holder to a proportional claim on the company’s assets and profits. The Dutch East India Company (VOC) issued the first recognized share certificates in 1606, pioneering the model of dispersed corporate ownership that underpins modern capital markets. Stock certificates in this collection span colonial trading companies, national railways, banks, and industrial enterprises.

Browse stock certificates in the collection →

Tontine

A collective investment scheme in which subscribers contribute to a shared fund and receive annual payments; as members die, their income share is redistributed among the survivors, with the last survivor eventually claiming the full fund. Named after Neapolitan banker Lorenzo de Tonti, tontines were widely used by European governments in the 17th and 18th centuries as a means of raising long-term capital, blurring the boundary between investment and life insurance.

Browse tontines in the collection →
